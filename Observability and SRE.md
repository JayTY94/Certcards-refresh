Impact-based Alerting vs Symptom-based Alerting
Impact-based alerting triggers when users are actually experiencing problems, such as elevated error rates or failed requests. Symptom-based alerting triggers on internal signals like CPU usage, memory pressure, or pod restarts that may correlate with failures but do not necessarily indicate user impact. Mature SRE practices prefer impact-based alerts for paging to reduce alert fatigue.

An Error Budget is the allowable amount of unreliability a system can experience over a defined period while still meeting its Service Level Objective (SLO). It represents the tradeoff space between reliability and feature velocity, enabling teams to decide when to slow releases or invest in stability work.
........
Error Budget Burn Rate measures how quickly a service is consuming its error budget relative to the allowed rate. A high burn rate indicates that reliability is being lost faster than acceptable and may require immediate action, even if the absolute number of failures is still small.
........
Time-weighted Reliability means that when failures occur matters, not just how many occur. Multiple small outages spread over time can consume error budgets faster than a single larger outage, which is why frequent intermittent failures are treated as higher risk in SRE practices.
........
Rolling Window SLOs evaluate reliability over a continuously moving time window (for example, the last 30 days) rather than a fixed calendar period. This prevents teams from “resetting” reliability after a bad period and ensures SLOs reflect current system behavior.
........
Observability is the ability to understand why a system is behaving a certain way based on the signals it emits. Unlike monitoring, which answers whether something is wrong, observability enables investigation of unknown or novel failure modes by correlating metrics, logs, and traces.
........
An Unknown-Unknown is a failure mode that was not anticipated and therefore has no predefined alert or dashboard. Observability is designed to support investigation of unknown-unknowns by enabling flexible querying and correlation rather than relying solely on predefined checks.
........
High Cardinality refers to metrics or dimensions that have many possible values, such as user IDs, request IDs, regions, or feature flags. High-cardinality data enables precise slicing of telemetry to identify which users, services, or conditions are affected during an incident.
........
Latent Conditions are hidden weaknesses in a system that do not cause failures on their own but contribute to incidents when combined with other factors. These conditions often accumulate silently and are a key focus of blameless postmortems.
........
Systems Thinking in Incident Analysis treats failures as the result of interactions between multiple components rather than a single root cause. When no clear root cause exists, it usually indicates systemic design weaknesses rather than missing data or individual mistakes.
........
Blast Radius describes the scope of impact when a failure occurs, including how many users, services, or regions are affected. Observability practices focus on rapidly determining blast radius to guide response priority and communication.
........
Traffic Volume is a contextual metric that describes how much demand a system is handling, but it is not itself a signal of health. Traffic is typically used as a denominator for error rates or latency analysis rather than as a paging indicator.
........
Signal vs Context
A signal directly indicates system correctness or user experience, such as error rate or request success. Contextual metrics, like traffic volume or CPU usage, provide background information but are not sufficient on their own to determine whether a system is failing.
........
Symptom-based alerting triggers on indirect indicators of potential problems, such as high CPU usage, memory pressure, or pod restarts. These alerts often correlate with failures but do not guarantee user impact and can lead to alert fatigue if used for paging.
........
Impact-based alerting triggers when users are actually experiencing degraded service, such as increased error rates or failed requests. This form of alerting is preferred for paging because it aligns operational response with real customer pain.
........
Error budget consumption describes how much of the allowed unreliability has already been used within an SLO period. It focuses on how reliability is spent over time, not just whether the SLO has been violated.
........
Time-weighted failures recognize that frequent, intermittent failures often degrade user trust more than a single sustained outage. As a result, many short incidents can be more damaging to reliability objectives than one longer failure.
........
Rolling window evaluation continuously measures reliability over a moving time range, such as “the last 30 days.” This prevents reliability from appearing artificially healthy after a bad period simply because a calendar boundary was crossed.
........
Fixed window evaluation measures reliability over static periods like calendar months. This approach can hide recent instability and encourages unhealthy behavior such as deferring fixes until the window resets.
........
Incident vs Failure
A failure is a technical malfunction in a system component, while an incident is a situation that requires coordinated human response. Not all failures become incidents, but all incidents involve failures that matter operationally.
........
Latent conditions are hidden weaknesses in a system that remain dormant until combined with other factors. These conditions accumulate over time and are often the true drivers behind major incidents.
........
Multiple contributing causes describe incidents where no single change or fault is sufficient to explain the failure. Instead, the incident emerges from the interaction of several necessary conditions within the system.
........
Observability vs Monitoring
Monitoring answers known questions such as “Is the service up?” Observability enables investigation into unknown or unexpected behavior by allowing engineers to ask new questions of the system’s telemetry.
........
High cardinality dimensions are telemetry attributes with many possible values, such as user IDs, request IDs, or regions. They enable precise analysis of blast radius and selective impact during incidents.
........
Blast radius describes the scope and reach of an incident, including how many users, services, or regions are affected. Rapidly identifying blast radius is critical for prioritizing response and communication.
........
Traffic volume is most useful when used as a denominator for metrics like error rate or latency percentiles. On its own, traffic does not indicate health, but it provides essential context for interpreting other signals.
